\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Recent research in computer vision has addressed inspection and change detection problems across a wide range of domains, including remote sensing, building inspection, indoor scene understanding, urban monitoring, medical imaging, and insurance claim assessment. There is a clear shift across fields from classical machine learning toward deep learning approaches, primarily convolutional neural networks (CNN) and, more recently, foundational and vision language models. These methods are effectively adopted in detecting defects, damages, and anomalies in the sources like image data.

Despite these advances, a large research gap remains after exploring 11 research gaps in papers. The existing approaches focus on single image analysis and do not model temporal change. Semantic comparison of condition images has seen little adoption beyond medical and remote sensing applications. Current methods produce detection or classification outputs without translating results into inspection oriented decision support. As a result, there is no integrated approach for indoor property inspections that combines temporal image comparison with inspection preparation or prioritization.

This thesis addresses this gap by investigating image based comparison of pre- and post-lease property images within the rental housing context. By focusing on temporal change detection in indoor environments and linking model outputs to inspection decision making, the proposed approach aims to improve efficiency, consistency, and objectivity in housing inspections.

\textbf{Problem Statement}

While automatic inspection tools are explored, approaches for image-based property condition assessment remain absent in housing management. Current practices depend on physical inspections and tenant reports, resulting in limited, not frequent insight into property condition. This gap constrains both efficiency and data based decision making in housing corporations such as De Goede Woning.

\textbf{Key Research Question}

\emph{To what extent can a Siamese computer vision approach based on foundation-model embeddings be used to compare lease property images and detect and classify inspection relevant changes such as damage, alterations, and wear and tear? }

\textbf{Sub-Research Questions}
\begin{itemize}
    \item How does a Siamese embedding-based change detection approach perform compared to baseline image similarity methods when applied to lease property images? 
    \item How can differences between Siamese image embeddings be used to distinguish and classify types of property changes, including damage, alterations, wear and tear, and maintenance needs? 
    \item To what extent is the Siamese approach robust to variations in lighting, camera angle, surface appearance, and image quality commonly present in tenant-provided images? 
    \item How can the outputs of change detection models be translated into inspection oriented indicators that support housing staff in deciding whether a physical inspection is necessary? 

\end{itemize}

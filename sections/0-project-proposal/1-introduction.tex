\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Recent discovery's in computer vision have enabled automated inspection across domains such as remote sensing, building assessment, indoor scene understanding, urban monitoring, and medical imaging. These fields rely on deep learning convolutional neural networks and, more recently, foundation-model and vision language approaches to detect defects, anomalies, and structural changes. Earlier work, including the review by Cheng, used classical classifiers, while newer studies explore U-Nets, R-CNN variants, YOLO architectures, and limited applications of Siamese networks. Yet Siamese modelling is still concentrated in medical imaging, where longitudinal datasets like MSSEG2\cite{MSSEG2_dataset} Kamraoui et al.\cite{Kamraoui_Mansencal_Manjon_Coupé_2022} provide, standardized image pairs.

This focus on medical data introduces a broader gap. Siamese methods are rarely applied in settings where images are scarce, heterogeneous, and unaligned conditions common in house inspections, military surface-change monitoring, cadastral boundary shifts, and other low data change detection tasks. Although MSSEG2\cite{MSSEG2_dataset} is methodologically instructive, its imaging conditions and high-quality annotations make it unsuitable for real-world house inspections. Consequently, the field lacks a method for robust Siamese-based temporal comparison on small, unstructured image sets and make it equally accurate.

This thesis investigates whether an embedded Siamese approach can support pre- and post-lease change assessment in house inspections under uncontrolled visual conditions.

\textbf{Problem Statement}

Automated image-based condition assessment is largely absent in the domain of housing management. Reliance on physical inspections provides inconsistent data, limiting the ability of organizations such as De Goede Woning to detect changes over time and make systematic, evidence-based decisions.

\textbf{Key Research Question}

\emph{To what extent can a Siamese computer vision approach based on foundation-model embeddings be used to compare lease property images and detect and classify inspection relevant changes such as damage, alterations, and wear and tear? }

\textbf{Sub-Research Questions}
\begin{itemize}
    \item How does a Siamese embedding-based change detection approach perform compared to baseline image similarity methods when applied to lease property images? 
    \item How can differences between Siamese image embeddings be used to distinguish and classify types of property changes, including damage, alterations, wear and tear, and maintenance needs? 
    \item To what extent is the Siamese approach robust to variations in lighting, camera angle, surface appearance, and image quality commonly present in tenant-provided images? 
    \item How can the outputs of change detection models be translated into inspection oriented indicators that support housing staff in deciding whether a physical inspection is necessary? 

\end{itemize}
